\section{Surrogate Criteria Along the Threshold-Count Axis}
\label{sec:r2}

That reconstruction-fidelity surrogates are biased as segmentation criteria was
established on natural images by Hegazy and Gabr~\cite{hegazy_metricbias} and, for
PSNR specifically including the multi-level case, earlier still. We claim the
domain extension, not the discovery: we move the question to clinical ground
truth on brain MRI and to the axis along which the field selects model
complexity, the number of thresholds $k$.

\subsection{An Analytic Argument, Not Only a Correlation}

% Prove it, don't correlate it (A4a): PSNR is a monotone function of the
% quantization error, so it increases with k BY CONSTRUCTION (Lloyd-Max). An
% analytic argument cannot be defeated by sample size.
PSNR is a monotone function of the mean squared quantization error of the
piecewise-constant reconstruction induced by the thresholds. Adding a threshold
refines that quantization and cannot increase the squared error, so PSNR is
non-decreasing in $k$ by construction, independently of any segmentation quality
--- the same Lloyd--Max property that makes finer quantizers reconstruct better.
The argument is analytic and cannot be defeated by sample size; the data merely
confirm it. Mean PSNR rises monotonically across every $k$ step on all four
target-by-background settings, from $21.5$--$25.0$ dB at $k=2$ to $36.5$--$36.9$
dB at $k=10$, and the PSNR-optimal threshold count is the largest available,
$k=10$, in $16$ of $16$ configurations. The Dice-optimal count, by contrast, is
$2$ or $3$, and never $10$ outside a single degenerate cell. The argmax of the
reported surrogate and the argmax of clinical quality disagree universally and
by construction.

\subsection{The Cost of Selecting the Threshold Count by a Surrogate}

% ⚠️ Selecting k is a LEARNED choice (prereg §6/A3, family B) => it may ONLY be
% evaluated out-of-fold. Selecting k on the test patients would be exactly the
% error this paper documents.
The clinical cost of selecting $k$ by the surrogate is the per-patient Dice
difference between the Dice-optimal and the PSNR-optimal threshold count. Holding
the optimizer at the exact global optimum, so that the effect cannot be an
artifact of any metaheuristic, this cost is significant but its magnitude is
governed by the decoding rule. On whole tumor it is a median $0.126$ Dice under
the primary \texttt{brightest} rule with the intensity-zero background excluded,
$0.102$ with it included, and reaches $0.321$ under morphological decoding; on
enhancing tumor under \texttt{brightest} the two argmaxes coincide and the cost
is zero. Across all decoding rules the per-patient cost spans $0.0009$ to
$0.3210$ Dice --- more than two orders of magnitude, in which the only thing that
varies is the Stage-2 decoder, not the optimizer, the objective, or $k$. The cost
of the surrogate is real, and it is set downstream of the optimization the
surrogate scores.

\subsection{What This Does Not Show}

% Written BEFORE the numbers, on purpose: it is the paragraph that survives
% adversarial reading.
Within a fixed threshold count, the association between surrogate scores and the
clinical score across algorithms is positive: the per-$k$ Spearman correlation
between PSNR and Dice across methods reaches $+0.89$. A reviewer can reproduce
this in minutes, and we state it plainly. It is not in tension with the result
above. A positive association among competing solutions at one complexity, and an
opposite trend as complexity itself changes, are a textbook aggregation effect;
the disagreement we report is on the $k$ axis, not the algorithm axis.

We also report what does not survive. The pre-registered secondary form of this
section --- a per-patient rank correlation $\rho(k,\mathrm{Dice}) < -0.5$ ---
fails in all $16$ configurations, and its sign flips with the decoding rule, from
$-0.94$ under morphological decoding to $+0.29$ under band-union decoding on whole
tumor, and to $+0.99$ under \texttt{brightest} on enhancing tumor. The plain
statement ``higher $k$ gives lower Dice'' is therefore not supported in general
and we withdraw it. What survives is the load-bearing claim, and it is the one
that is analytic rather than correlational: the surrogate used to choose model
complexity is structurally maximized at the wrong value of $k$, and the clinical
cost of following it is set by the decoder.
