\section{Decomposing the Ceiling}
\label{sec:ceiling}

\subsection{What Is Already Known}

The existence of an upper bound on thresholding-based segmentation of brain MRI
is an established result, reported on BraTS FLAIR by François and
Tinarrage~\cite{francois_jmiv}. We do not restate it as a finding of ours. Our
question is different: \emph{what does that bound consist of?}

\subsection{The Tightest Intensity-Only Bound}

% APPLICATION, not mathematics. Attribute in the same breath as the statement.
For a fixed image, the mask that maximizes Dice among all masks defined by an
arbitrary set of gray levels is obtained by ordering gray levels by the purity
of their voxels with respect to the reference mask and taking the best prefix;
this is the F1-threshold result of Lipton \emph{et al.}~\cite{lipton_f1}
transposed to the segmentation setting, where Dice coincides with F1 and purity
plays the role of a calibrated posterior. We claim no mathematical novelty for
it. Its consequence here is practical: the bound is computable in $O(L \log L)$
from a histogram, so the attainable performance of the entire thresholding
family is knowable before any optimizer is written.

\subsection{The Ladder}

We place every method on a single Dice axis over the same held-out patients
(Table~\ref{tab:ceiling}). The rungs, from the bottom, are the unsupervised
per-image thresholds and clustering baselines (Otsu, Li, triangle, $k$-means,
GMM); the out-of-fold single-parameter percentile threshold; the contiguous-band
oracle; the arbitrary-gray-level-set oracle; and a 2-D U-Net on identical
single-slice input. The three oracle rungs use test-time ground truth and are
labeled as such throughout: they are unreachable upper bounds, not methods. On
whole tumor the classical thresholds sit near $0.29$ Dice, the exact-optimum
methods at the primary decoding are far below their own intensity ceiling, the
contiguous-band and gray-level-set oracles sit at $0.848$ and $0.853$, and the
learned model sits above all of them at $0.923$.

\subsection{Decomposition}

The decomposition is the contribution, and it reads directly off the ladder as
two gaps on whole tumor. The first, from the contiguous-band oracle to the
arbitrary-gray-level-set oracle, is what is lost by insisting on a single
intensity interval: it is small, $0.848$ to $0.853$, about $0.005$ Dice. The
second, from the gray-level-set oracle to the learned model on identical input,
is what is absent from intensity but present in the pixels: it is large. The
U-Net exceeds the intensity ceiling by a median $0.050$ Dice ($0.9234$ versus
$0.8532$; paired bootstrap $95\%$ CI $[0.041, 0.065]$), winning on $293$ of $368$
patients ($79.6\%$), with $p = 2.8\times10^{-32}$ and a rank-biserial effect size
of $0.71$, consistent across all five folds. Because the U-Net reads the same
single FLAIR slice the thresholding methods read, the roughly five Dice points it
recovers cannot be information the input lacks; they are information the
intensity-only hypothesis class cannot express. The shortfall of thresholding on
this target is not a limit of the representation's information but of its
form.

\subsection{Where the Representational Limit Is Provably Real}

The whole-tumor target is the favorable case for the methods under audit, which
is why we keep it as the primary and read the decomposition there. The hard
target makes the representational limit visible without a learned model:
enhancing tumor on T1ce is a bright rim around a dark core, a shape no single
contiguous intensity band can represent, and its intensity ceiling is
correspondingly low --- the gray-level-set oracle reaches only $0.635$ Dice
(Table~\ref{tab:ceiling}). Where whole tumor shows that intensity thresholding
leaves recoverable information on the table, enhancing tumor shows a target whose
structure intensity cannot reach at all. Extending the learned rung to this
target, whose input protocol our current model does not cover, is left to future
work; the intensity ceiling already bounds what any thresholding method can do
there.

We note the preregistered branch for completeness: had the learned model
\emph{not} exceeded the intensity ceiling, the conclusion would not have
retreated to ``thresholding saturates'' --- that bound is already
published~\cite{francois_jmiv} --- but would have read that the ceiling is high
and the failure is one of threshold selection, which no metaheuristic in this
line addresses. The data took the other branch, and the decomposition stands.
